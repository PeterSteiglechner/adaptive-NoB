Exploring adaptive belief networks


Our beliefs about political issues are not entirely independent of each other; they are embedded and structured within personal belief systems. For example, Alice's concern about climate change may constrain her position on economic growth; however Bob similar level of concern about climate change might not conflict at all with his support for technological advances to foster economic growth. We can model these individual-level systems as networks of beliefs: nodes represent the individual's beliefs on selected issues and edges represent (positive or negative) relations, influences, or constraints between the belief items. Recent research has primarily explored how people change (or resist changing) their beliefs, given that they are structured in a network. These studies typically assumed that people’s belief networks are prescribed (for example, based on logical relation), static, and homogeneous. Much less work has focused on how the network structures themselves adapt over time and how individuals' belief system structures potentially diverge.

We propose a computational model to explore how people update their personal belief network structure simultaneously to their beliefs and how these additional dynamics affect differential responses and resistance to interventions or external influence. Our model extends the Networks of Beliefs (NB) theory, presented by Dalege, Olsson and Galesic (2025). The original theory described how individuals update their beliefs when they experience dissonance (either with other personal beliefs, with social beliefs or between social beliefs and external signals) within a statistical physics framework. In our extension, individuals also update their personal belief network structure by changing the edge weights connecting different personal beliefs. We base the updating of edges on two processes. The first represents social conformity – when others see a strong (or no) relation between belief i and belief j, it is difficult to ignore (or maintain) this relation individually. The second process represents internal learning – if two beliefs of an individual consistently change in the same direction, the person will draw a relation between these belief items. 

In this contribution, we explore how changes in belief networks may lead to heterogeneity among individuals, and how this affects polarisation and consensus patterns: Does plasticity of belief networks (and, thus, heterogeneity in peoples' belief networks) deepen polarisation? Furthermore, we explore how changes affect individuals' responses to interventions of external pressures: Can heterogeneity explain why some people respond to belief interventions while others beliefs (and belief systems) remain robust? 

In the following, we describe the model in more detail. Each agent in the model has beliefs, $x\in\{-1, -0.67, -0.33, 0, 0.33, 0.67, 1\}^10$, indicating agreement or disagreement with ten belief items. One of these belief items is the \textit{focal belief}. You can think of this for example as having nine hidden or more abstract beliefs about the extent of climate change, the causes of it, and the responsibility for it and having one particular focal belief, e.g. about the value of a carbon tax, that people eventually debate and observe in others. We intialise belief networks as fully connected, i.e.\ each belief i is connected to every other belief j with edge weight $\omega_{ij} = \omega_0$. The focal beliefs are in addition connected to others' focal beliefs with edge weight $\rho$. A relation between beliefs can contribute to dissonance or reassurance if the beliefs are misaligned or aligned. Using the analogy of energy in spin systems, we define the dissonance between beliefs i and j as $H_{pers, ij} = \omega_{ij} \cdot x_i \cdot x_j$ or, in sum, $H_{pers} = \sum_{ij} \omega_{ij} \cdot x_i \cdot x_j$ for links between personal beliefs and $H_{soc} = \sum_{{\rm network \ neighbours\ } nb} \rho \cdot x_{\rm focal} \cdot x^{\rm nb}_{\rm focal}$ for the edges connecting the focal belief to focal beliefs of other people (organised in a social network). Individuals update beliefs based on the dissonance $H = H_{\rm pers} + H_{\rm soc}$ in a way that they consider all possible beliefs on a given item i and choose a p


We formalise the internal process as Hebbian learning: individuals learn to strengthen those relations that are already consistent with their personal belief networks and unlearn or reverse those relations that are inconsistent. We formalise social learning as a linear convergence of the personal belief network towards the social belief network: individuals observe the beliefs of their peers, infer how these beliefs are correlated with each other, and align their personal belief networks accordingly. This reflects how difficult it is for individuals to unlearn (or ignore) dissonant relations when they are frequently reminded of such dissonance during social interactions. It also reflects how social learning can strengthen individuals belief networks and enhance their sense of coherence if their personal belief networks are consistent with social observations.
